Document of Freedom

Review of A Familiar Exposition of the Constitution of the United States by Joseph Story.

This book, written in 1840, is an enlghtening commentary on the Constitution of the of the United States by a renowned nineteenth-century legal scholar.

Even if you have carefully read the entire Constitution, you will find it edifying to go through it again, as you take in Story's explanation of the exact meaning of each clause and the reason behind its inclusion.

Above all, you will readily grasp the essential purpose of the Constitution: the creation of a system to limit government's power and thereby to protect the liberty of the citizens. (Amazingly, this book was intended as a high school text.)

The author shows how the Framers, with an extensive knowledge of the history of governments, sought to separate and balance the powers of the three branches ofgovern- ment. Story's wealth of insights ranges from .why the Constitution contains an explicit defmition of treason to how the Constitution creates a check, not just on government, but also on the tyranny of the majority.

Unlike many modern intellectuals, Story recognizes that the Constitution was designed to protect the right to property and to contracts. He realizes, for instance, that the power to regulate commerce was granted to the federal government, not to allow Washington to control the economy, but to prevent senseless economic regulation and "trade wars" among the states.

Story writes, in conclusion: "If this work shall but inspire the rising generation with a more ardent love of their country, and unquenchable thirst for liberty, and a profound reverence for the Constitution and the Union, then it will have accomplished all that its author ought to desire." To an admirable extent, Story succeeds.
